# Resistance Is Rational

## Why Organisations Fight AI Adoption, and Why They Are Right To

By Gerhard Diedericks
[www.bayon.ai](https://www.bayon.ai)

*March 2026*

---

*Incumbents resist AI adoption not because they are stupid or Luddite, but because resistance is economically rational when your business model depends on managing scarcity. The moment you understand this, you stop moralising and start navigating.*

---

## The misdiagnosis

The standard narrative about AI resistance goes like this: organisations are slow to adopt AI because leaders are afraid, ignorant, or stuck in their ways. If only they could see the opportunity, they would embrace the change.

This narrative is wrong. Not because fear and ignorance play no role, but because it misidentifies the mechanism. Most organisational resistance to AI is not irrational. It is the opposite. It is a perfectly calibrated response to a structural threat.

If your organisation, profession, or platform is built on managing scarcity, then AI-driven cost compression threatens not only revenue but identity, authority, and institutional legitimacy. The natural response is not surrender but defence. And that defence is rational because, from inside the current system, the economics support it.

Understanding this changes everything about how you approach AI transformation. You stop treating resistance as a people problem and start treating it as a structural one.

---

## The logic of defence

When AI begins compressing the cost structure of a domain, incumbents typically respond with some combination of the following:

Lobbying for regulation that preserves existing structures. Tightening credential requirements to maintain professional barriers. Reintroducing complexity around tasks that AI has simplified. Expanding liability frameworks to make AI adoption legally risky. Reinforcing professional credentials as markers of "real" expertise. Enclosing infrastructure to control access. Repositioning into premium residual segments where AI cannot easily follow.

This behaviour is not necessarily malicious. Systems often respond this way when their core economics face pressure. A law firm that has built its revenue model on billing hours for document review does not resist AI document review because it hates technology. It resists because AI document review threatens the economic foundation of the firm. The resistance is proportional to the threat, which is precisely what rational behaviour looks like.

That is why resistance must be anticipated rather than moralised. Telling people their rational economic behaviour is "fear of change" does not help them navigate. It alienates them.

## The four-phase pattern

Across many domains, the transition from resistance to release follows a recognisable sequence. This is not a prediction for any specific industry, but a pattern that recurs often enough to be useful as a navigational tool.

**Phase 1: Compression begins.** AI starts undercutting the cost structure at the edges. Early use cases look narrow. Incumbents remain profitable. The core model appears intact. But cracks appear at the margins. A junior analyst's work can be done in minutes rather than days. A standard contract can be drafted by a machine. The effects are visible but containable.

**Phase 2: The middle layer drains.** Roles that primarily manage scarcity, rather than create value, begin losing economic rationale. Routine review, standard drafting, standardised analysis, and basic coordination thin out first. This is cognitive scarcity being dissolved at the middle layer of the organisational pyramid. The middle layer exists because the scarce thing at the top (expertise, judgement, creative vision) cannot reach everyone at the base directly. When AI compresses those coordination costs, the middle layer's reason to exist weakens.

**Phase 3: Defence inversion.** This is the threshold moment. The cost of maintaining defence begins to approach or exceed the rent it protects. The strategic question changes from "How do we stop this?" to "What is our cheapest defensible residual position?" This moment marks when defence stops being profitable preservation and becomes expensive delay. Recognising when you have crossed from Phase 2 to Phase 3 is one of the most important strategic judgements in the AI transition.

**Phase 4: Release.** The market lets go, not morally but economically. Incumbents retreat to residual layers: complex edge cases, premium service, brand trust, liability-bearing roles, deep human judgement. The mass market moves towards AI-enabled alternatives. Incumbents rarely release because they are persuaded. They release when continued defence no longer pays.

---

## The pattern in practice

This sequence is not unique to AI. Recognising where it has played out calibrates expectations for what comes next.

**Recorded music (1999 to 2015).** Distribution costs collapsed. The industry's response followed the pattern precisely: litigation, DRM, lobbying (defence). Defence inversion arrived when enforcement costs exceeded recoverable revenue. Release took the form of streaming, which offered cheaper monetisation than warfare. The residual: live performance, merchandise, direct fan relationships. Notably, value migrated to platform owners and away from mid-tier artists. This is capture in action.

**News and journalism (2005 to present).** Information distribution costs fell toward zero. The middle layer, including print logistics, classified advertising, and local monopolies, drained rapidly. The industry remains mid-transition: some outlets found subscription models, but local news collapsed and platform capture of advertising revenue is well documented. The valley here is severe and ongoing.

**Customer support (2023 to present).** AI is projected to handle 95% of customer interactions by 2025. Companies report 30% operational cost reductions. But the pattern shows capture dynamics: AI reduces the cost of producing support while proprietary platforms control access. The question is whether savings flow to users (lower prices, better service) or to infrastructure owners (higher margins, platform lock-in).

These cases suggest three things. The pattern is real, but speed varies based on regulatory structure, physical constraints, and incumbent coordination. Capture is the default outcome unless actively countered. And "release" often means a new equilibrium, not disappearance.

## What this means for transformation leaders

If you are running an AI transformation programme and treating resistance as irrational, you are working against the grain of economic logic. Here is what follows from taking resistance seriously.

**Map the scarcity structure first.** Before asking "how do we adopt AI?", ask "what scarcity does our current model depend on?" If the answer is cognitive scarcity (analysis, drafting, review, standard decision-making), the compression is coming whether you prepare for it or not. If the answer is physical or human scarcity, you have more time but different challenges.

**Name the phase honestly.** Where is your domain in the four-phase pattern? If you are in Phase 1, you have time to design the transition. If you are in Phase 2, the middle layer is already thinning and the decisions you make now will determine whether you end up in a residual position or a transformed one. If you are approaching Phase 3, the economics of defence are about to invert and delay becomes more expensive than adaptation.

**Design for the residual.** The question is not "how do we stop AI from changing things?" The question is "what does the post-compression shape look like, and where do we want to be in it?" Different domains will settle into different configurations: flattened pyramids, networked meshes, hourglasses, hybrid stacks. The strategic question is which shape your domain is moving towards and where human scarcity genuinely sits within it.

**Do not moralise the resistance.** The people resisting AI adoption in your organisation are often the ones who understand the current model most clearly. Their resistance contains signal about what the model actually depends on. Listen to it before you try to override it.

---

## The deeper insight

The four-phase pattern reveals something important about the nature of the AI transition itself. This is not a technology adoption challenge. It is a structural economic transition in which entire layers of economic activity are losing their rationale.

The organisations that navigate this well will not be the ones that adopted AI fastest. They will be the ones that understood what they were standing on, recognised when the ground shifted, and repositioned toward the layers of value that remain durable after the compression is complete.

Resistance is rational. But so is knowing when to let go.
