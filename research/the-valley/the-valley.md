# The Valley

## The Painful Gap Between Old Structures and New Ones

By Gerhard Diedericks
[www.bayon.ai](https://www.bayon.ai)

*March 2026*

---

*Between the old structures losing their rationale and the new structures becoming reliable, there is a valley. It is real, it is painful, and it is unevenly distributed. Pretending the crossing is smooth helps no one.*

---

## What the valley looks like

A serious map of the AI transition must be honest about the gap. The enthusiasts promise abundance. The denialists promise continuity. Neither is telling the truth about what happens in between.

The valley looks like this:

Cognitive-scarcity job compression before new roles stabilise. The old jobs are thinning. The new jobs are not yet defined. The people losing the first are not the people gaining the second.

Identity disruption as professionals discover their expertise is more cognitive than human. This is the sharpest cut. You spent fifteen years building expertise in something that a well-prompted AI system can now do in minutes. The discovery is not merely economic. It is personal.

Institutional confusion and policy lag. The structures that are supposed to help, education systems, professional bodies, government agencies, were designed for the previous era and have not yet adapted.

Widening gaps between early adopters and everyone else. Some organisations and individuals are already navigating the transition. Many are not. The gap between the two groups is widening.

Declining trust in existing pathways. Education, careers, credentials: the routes that used to lead somewhere reliable are becoming uncertain.

Rising anxiety and political volatility. When people feel the ground shifting and the institutions are not helping, the political consequences are predictable.

---

## The numbers behind the gap

The World Economic Forum projects 85 million jobs displaced by 2025, with 97 million new roles emerging, a net positive of 12 million globally. But these are not one-to-one swaps. The new roles are not in the same locations. They do not require the same skills. They will not go to the same people.

A net positive tells you nothing about who falls into the valley on the way to the other side.

Unemployment among 20 to 30 year-olds in tech-exposed occupations has risen by almost 3 percentage points since early 2025. These are the canaries. They entered the workforce with skills that the market valued two years ago and is commoditising now. They did everything right by the old map. The old map is being redrawn.

---

## The identity layer

The economic disruption is visible. The identity disruption is less discussed but more damaging.

Most knowledge workers have built not just careers but identities around cognitive capabilities: their ability to analyse, synthesise, draft, review, navigate complex information. These capabilities were genuine. They were hard-won. They were the basis of professional self-respect.

The four-scarcity framework reveals that much of what we called "expertise" sits in the cognitive scarcity category rather than the human scarcity category. AI is not just automating tasks. It is revealing that the tasks were more reproducible than the professionals performing them believed.

This discovery is an identity crisis, not a tooling adjustment. Telling someone to "upskill" or "adapt" without acknowledging what they are losing is not helpful. It is dismissive.

The valley is a grief process as much as an economic transition.

---

## What makes the valley worse

The valley worsens under specific conditions.

When institutions deny the transition. If the organisations responsible for education, regulation, and workforce development pretend the change is not happening, the people who depend on those institutions are left without support at the moment they need it most.

When incumbents defend rent while underinvesting in alternatives. When the energy goes into preserving the existing model rather than building the next one, the valley gets deeper and longer.

When markets are assumed to produce equitable outcomes by default. "Let the market decide" sounds neutral. It is not. Markets decide in favour of whoever can shape the rules, and during transitions, that is usually the concentrated beneficiaries of the old system, not the dispersed individuals trying to navigate to the new one.

---

## What navigating the valley looks like

The valley is an argument for better navigation, not for pretending the crossing is smooth. Some principles for organisations navigating it:

Acknowledge the loss before selling the gain. People will not engage with what comes next until they feel that what they are losing has been seen. This is not soft. It is structural. Denial creates resistance.

Build bridges, not lifeboats. Lifeboats are for when the ship is sinking. Bridges connect the old structure to the new one. The difference is in the design assumption: do you believe the people currently in the valley can make it to the other side, or are you writing them off?

Invest in human scarcity, not cognitive replacement. The professionals who thrive through this transition will be those who repositioned toward the human-scarcity layer: judgement under uncertainty, trust-dependent relationships, creative reframing, moral reasoning. The organisations that help their people make this shift will retain the talent. The ones that simply automate will lose it.

Design for the post-compression shape. The valley ends when the new structure stabilises. The organisations that are already designing for what comes after the compression, whether that is a flattened pyramid, a networked mesh, an hourglass, or a hybrid stack, will arrive on the other side with an operating model that works.

---

## The honest position

The valley is real. It will not be smooth. It will be worse for some than for others, and the distribution will not be fair.

The honest response is not denial, nor panic, but clear-eyed navigation: see the valley for what it is, acknowledge what is being lost, and build toward the structures that will hold on the other side.
