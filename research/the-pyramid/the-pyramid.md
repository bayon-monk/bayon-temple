# The Pyramid

## How AI Reshapes Every Industry's Operating Structure

By Gerhard Diedericks
[www.bayon.ai](https://www.bayon.ai)

*March 2026*

---

*Most industries are pyramids built around a scarce resource. AI compresses the middle layer. The question is not whether this happens, but what shape your domain takes afterwards, and where you want to be in it.*

---

## The structure you are standing in

Most people have never thought about the shape of the industry they work in. But the shape matters, because it determines where you sit and what happens when the ground shifts.

The vast majority of industries, professions, and institutions are pyramids. Not by design choice, but because they were built to solve the same underlying problem: how do you get something scarce to the people who need it?

The pyramid has three layers.

**The top layer** contains a relatively small group who create or embody genuinely scarce value. Experts, innovators, practitioners whose judgement cannot be easily replicated. The surgeon who can perform a procedure no one else can. The lawyer whose courtroom instinct was built across decades. The designer whose creative vision reframes how a product works. These people sit at the top because what they do is hard to reproduce.

**The middle layer** is much larger. It consists of the people, processes, and institutions that manage access to the scarce value at scale. This layer performs real work: coordination, verification, routing, quality control, and trust functions. But much of it exists because the scarce thing at the top cannot reach everyone at the base directly. In the language of transaction cost economics, the middle layer exists because transaction costs are high. That is where the system's friction sits.

**The base layer** is largest: the people and organisations who need access to the value at the top. In the scarcity model, the base experiences value primarily through the middle layer's mediation. Access is rationed by cost, credentials, geography, and gatekeeping.

This structure is so universal it becomes invisible. Legal services, healthcare, education, financial advice, consulting, journalism, software development: all pyramids. All built around the same logic. All about to face the same pressure.

---

## What happens when the middle compresses

When AI compresses the cost of middle-layer functions, three things happen simultaneously.

First, the economic rationale for large parts of the middle layer weakens. If AI can do routine analysis, standard drafting, basic coordination, and procedural navigation at a fraction of the cost and time, then the roles that primarily perform these functions lose their reason to exist. This is not a value judgement about the people in those roles. It is an observation about economic structure.

Second, the top layer's reach can extend without proportional middle-layer growth. An expert whose insights previously required a team of intermediaries to distribute can now reach a much larger audience directly. A specialist doctor's diagnostic reasoning can be embedded in an AI system that reaches millions rather than the hundreds who could get an appointment.

Third, the base layer can potentially access value more directly. The patient who could not get a specialist referral. The small business that could not afford a strategy consultant. The student who could not access personalised tutoring. When the middle layer compresses, these barriers weaken.

This is why the AI transition feels more destabilising than a tooling upgrade. The question is not just how work gets done, but which organisational layers still make economic sense.

Early evidence suggests compression is already visible. Computer and mathematical occupations, among the most AI-exposed, saw some of the steepest unemployment rises between 2022 and 2025. Unemployment among 20 to 30 year-olds in tech-exposed occupations has risen by almost 3 percentage points since early 2025. These are early signals of middle-layer compression in action.

## Four shapes after disruption

What does the pyramid look like after the compression? The answer depends on the domain. Different industries will settle into different configurations, and recognising which shape your domain is moving towards is the first strategic question.

**The flattened pyramid.** The middle layer compresses dramatically while the top and base remain. Fewer intermediaries sit between expert and user. Legal information, educational content, and software tools may follow this pattern. The expert still matters, but the army of analysts, associates, and coordinators between the expert and the end user shrinks significantly.

**The networked mesh.** The pyramid dissolves into a more distributed structure. AI enables peer-to-peer coordination that previously required centralised management. Open-source communities already show elements of this pattern. Instead of a hierarchy routing value downward, you get a network where participants contribute and access value laterally. This shape emerges where the scarce resource was coordination itself.

**The hourglass.** The middle compresses while new bottlenecks form at the infrastructure layer. Value concentrates at both the top (human scarcity) and the bottom (physical scarcity), with a thin middle. Healthcare and energy may follow this pattern. The surgeon and the data centre both remain essential. The administrative and coordination layers between them thin dramatically.

**The hybrid stack.** Human-AI organisations develop a distinctive structure: small, high-judgement human teams at decision points, AI systems handling scale and coordination, and a thin integration layer between them. This is not a pyramid or a mesh. It is something new, built for the specific capabilities of both humans and AI systems working together.

## Seeing your own pyramid

The framework becomes useful when you apply it to your own domain.

Start with the top layer. What is the genuinely scarce value in your industry? Not what people claim is scarce, but what actually cannot be replicated by a well-resourced AI system operating at scale. Be rigorous about this. Much of what professionals call "expertise" is cognitive scarcity that AI is already dissolving: pattern recognition, precedent retrieval, standard analysis, procedural navigation. The genuinely scarce layer is typically narrower than the profession assumes.

Then map the middle. What percentage of the economic activity in your domain is coordination, mediation, gatekeeping, and quality control? How much of that exists because the scarce thing at the top could not reach the base directly? This is the layer under the most immediate pressure.

Then look at the base. Who needs access to the value at the top but currently cannot get it, or can only get it through expensive, slow, or gatekept channels? This is where the opportunity sits, because when the middle compresses, the base gains access.

Finally, ask the strategic question: which of the four shapes is your domain moving towards? And where do you want to be in the new configuration?

If you are in the top layer and your scarcity is genuinely human, your position may strengthen. Your reach extends. Your leverage increases. The AI transition amplifies you.

If you are in the middle layer, the question is urgent. Is your role primarily coordination (at risk) or primarily judgement under uncertainty (more durable)? The answer determines whether you need to move up-stack toward human scarcity or find a new position in the emerging structure.

If you serve the base, you may be about to see access expand dramatically. The organisations that figure out how to deliver top-layer value directly to the base, using AI to replace the middle-layer mediation, will define the next era.

---

## The capture risk

There is a complication. Middle-layer compression does not automatically mean broader access. Value that is released by compression can be captured rather than distributed.

If the platforms that deliver AI-mediated access to expertise control the infrastructure, they become the new middle layer. The pyramid does not flatten. It changes shape, with new gatekeepers at the infrastructure level rather than the coordination level. The base gains access to cheaper services but loses agency over how those services are delivered, priced, and controlled.

This is why understanding the pyramid is necessary but not sufficient. You also need to understand where value migrates when the middle compresses. That question, the question of capture, is addressed in the full working paper.

---

## The question to carry

Which shape is your domain moving towards? And where do you want to be in it?

This is not an abstract question. It is the most practical strategic question of the next decade. The professionals, organisations, and institutions that answer it clearly will design operating models that work after the compression is complete. The ones that do not will find themselves defending a position that has already lost its economic foundation.

The pyramid is not collapsing. It is reshaping. The question is whether you are shaping it or being shaped by it.
