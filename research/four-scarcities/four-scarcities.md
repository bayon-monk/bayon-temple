# The Four Scarcities

## Why AI Disrupts Everything at Different Speeds

By Gerhard Diedericks
[www.bayon.ai](https://www.bayon.ai)

*March 2026*

---

*Most people talk about AI as though it dissolves "scarcity" as a single thing. It does not. There are four kinds, each compressed at different rates, through different mechanisms, with different consequences. If you cannot distinguish between them, you cannot see where the real exposure is.*

---

## The problem with one word

The AI conversation is full of binary claims. AI will replace everything. AI will replace nothing. Abundance is coming. It is all hype.

These positions are not wrong because they are too extreme. They are wrong because they treat scarcity as one thing. When you collapse four distinct categories into a single word, you cannot see what is actually happening: rapid disruption in some domains, no movement in others, and a quiet migration of power that most people are not watching.

This essay introduces a framework for seeing more clearly. Four kinds of scarcity. Four different trajectories. One diagnostic you can apply to your own work in fifteen minutes.

---

## Cognitive scarcity

Scarcity tied to analytical capability, pattern recognition, knowledge retrieval, procedural expertise, and information processing. A junior analyst's ability to synthesise a dataset. A paralegal's knowledge of filing procedures. A consultant's capacity to produce a market overview. These were real skills, hard-won through years of training and experience.

This is the category AI is disrupting most visibly and most rapidly. The scarcity was genuine when these capabilities lived only in human minds, but it was a function of biological limitation, not permanent necessity. As AI replicates and often exceeds these capabilities at collapsing cost, cognitive scarcity is dissolving across virtually every knowledge domain simultaneously.

LLM inference prices have fallen by 9x to 900x per year depending on performance level, with the fastest declines since January 2024. Stanford's HAI Index documented a drop from $20 to $0.07 per million tokens for GPT-3.5-equivalent performance between November 2022 and October 2024. A 280-fold reduction in eighteen months.

Cognitive scarcity does not disappear entirely. Novel analytical challenges, cross-domain synthesis, and judgement calls at the frontier remain difficult. But the vast middle band of cognitive work, routine analysis, standard drafting, procedural navigation, pattern matching against known frameworks, is compressing rapidly.

---

## Manufactured scarcity

Scarcity created or maintained through institutional and commercial structures, often presented as quality control, safety, or professional necessity. Unnecessary credential barriers. Regulatory complexity that preserves intermediaries. Monopoly control over distribution. Artificial friction around routine services.

Manufactured scarcity is where AI deflation hits next. The cost structure was organisational or political rather than fundamentally productive. As cognitive scarcity weakens, the rationale for many manufactured barriers weakens with it. If AI can produce competent legal guidance, the argument for restricting access to legal information through professional gatekeeping becomes harder to sustain.

Not all professional structure is manufactured scarcity. A surgeon's training requirements reflect real physical constraints. A bridge engineer's certification protects lives. The question is whether the barrier serves the consumer or the gatekeeper. AI does not answer that question, but it makes it harder to avoid.

---

## Physical scarcity

Scarcity tied to material and thermodynamic reality. Land. Grid capacity. Cooling water. Specialised hardware. Energy infrastructure. Logistics. These remain physically constrained. AI may reduce friction around many things, but it does not dissolve the laws of physics. In some cases it intensifies pressure on them.

Physical scarcity is strategically important precisely because it persists while other forms weaken. As cognitive and manufactured scarcity compress, value migrates toward physical bottlenecks. The five largest hyperscalers are projected to spend over $600 billion on capital expenditure in 2026, with three companies now accounting for 58% of all hyperscale data centre capacity worldwide. This is not coincidence. It is capital following the scarcity gradient downward.

If you want to know where power is concentrating during the AI transition, do not look at who builds the models. Look at who controls the physical infrastructure the models run on.

---

## Human scarcity

The irreducible residual. What remains genuinely scarce after cognitive capability becomes abundant and manufactured barriers fall away. This is not "everything humans do." It is specifically what cannot be replicated even when AI can match or exceed human performance across most cognitive tasks.

Human scarcity includes: wisdom under genuine uncertainty, where the information is incomplete and the stakes are high. Trust built through sustained relationship and demonstrated integrity. Willpower and drive. Moral judgement that weighs competing values rather than optimising a single metric. Embodied presence and the forms of care, craft, and leadership that require physical participation. Creative vision that reframes problems rather than recombining existing solutions.

Human scarcity does not disappear, though its boundaries may shift as AI capabilities advance. The critical insight is that it is a much narrower category than most professionals currently assume. Much of what we have historically called "expertise" sits in the cognitive scarcity category rather than the human scarcity category. AI is revealing that distinction in real time.

---

## Why these four distinctions matter

AI-driven deflation affects these four forms at different rates and through different mechanisms. It dissolves cognitive scarcity directly and rapidly. It erodes the rationale for manufactured scarcity. It often leaves physical scarcity intact and sometimes makes it more strategically important. It does not touch human scarcity directly, though it changes the context in which human scarcity operates.

The framework reveals why the transition is so disorienting. Most professionals have built careers, identities, and economic positions on cognitive capabilities that felt permanent. The discovery that these capabilities are being commoditised is not merely an economic disruption. It is an identity disruption.

It also reveals where capture migrates. As cognitive and manufactured scarcity weaken, capture strategies move down-stack towards physical infrastructure: compute, chips, energy, data centres, and network infrastructure. If we do not distinguish between kinds of scarcity, we risk celebrating liberation at one layer while enclosure is quietly rebuilt at another.

## A self-diagnostic

Map your own role against the four categories. Be honest.

**What percentage of your working week is cognitive scarcity?** Routine analysis, standard drafting, information retrieval, procedural navigation, pattern matching against known frameworks. If a well-prompted AI system could do it at 80% of your quality, it counts.

**What percentage is manufactured scarcity?** Work that exists because of institutional structure, not because the task itself demands human involvement. Compliance for compliance's sake. Gatekeeping that protects position rather than quality. Process that persists because no one has challenged it.

**What percentage depends on physical scarcity?** Work tied to your physical presence, material handling, embodied skill, or the constraints of geography and infrastructure.

**What percentage is human scarcity?** Judgement under genuine uncertainty. Relationship-dependent trust. Creative reframing. Moral reasoning where values conflict. The work that remains necessary even in a world where AI can do the rest.

Most knowledge workers discover that 40 to 70 percent of their role sits in cognitive scarcity. Not because they lack talent, but because the economy was built to value cognitive capabilities that are now being reproduced at near-zero marginal cost.

The honest response is not denial. It is repositioning. Build toward the human-scarcity layer: the work that remains durable when the tools change. The professionals who thrive through this transition will be those who moved early, not because they ran from AI, but because they understood where the real value sits.

---

## The question beneath the question

The four-scarcity framework is a diagnostic tool, not a prediction. It does not tell you what will happen. It tells you what to look for.

When someone tells you AI will change everything, ask: which kind of scarcity? When someone tells you your job is safe, ask: which layer of it? When someone tells you the transition will be smooth, ask: for whom?

The organisations that navigate this transition well will be the ones that can see the four scarcities clearly, position themselves in human and physical scarcity rather than defending cognitive and manufactured positions, and design operating models that work after the compression is complete, not before.

The central problem of the AI transition is not automation. It is knowing what kind of scarcity you are standing on.
