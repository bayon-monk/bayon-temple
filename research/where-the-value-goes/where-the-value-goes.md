# Where the Value Goes

## Why AI Deflation Does Not Mean AI Liberation

By Gerhard Diedericks
[www.bayon.ai](https://www.bayon.ai)

*March 2026*

---

*Deflation does not make value disappear. It migrates. The central strategic question of the AI transition is not whether costs fall. It is where the released value flows.*

---

## The migration question

When AI compresses the cost of producing something, the savings do not vanish. They go somewhere. The question is where.

Two destinations are possible. The released value flows into broader access, lower friction, and larger commons. Or it flows into new chokepoints, tollbooths, and rent extraction systems.

This paper calls the second pattern capture.

The distinction that matters is between productive intermediation and extractive capture. Productive intermediation creates genuine value: a platform that makes something possible that was not possible before earns a legitimate return. Extractive capture intercepts value that AI deflation has already created, inserting rent-extracting friction between a near-zero-cost capability and the people who need it. The first deserves reward. The second deserves scrutiny.

---

## Why capture wins by default

Capture is not merely possible. It is the structurally favoured outcome unless actively countered.

The political economy is asymmetric. Those who benefit from capture, including infrastructure owners, platform operators, and incumbent gatekeepers, are concentrated, well-resourced, and organised. Those who would benefit from open access are diffuse, uncoordinated, and often unaware of what they are losing.

This is Mancur Olson's collective action problem in its purest form. A small group with large individual stakes will outorganise a large group with small individual stakes, even when the aggregate benefit to the larger group far exceeds the benefit to the smaller one.

The lobbying asymmetry is severe. Incumbents can afford to spend millions defending regulatory complexity that preserves their position. The diffuse public cannot coordinate equivalent pressure for simplification. The result: regulatory capture, complexity reintroduction, and procedural barriers that serve no one except those who profit from friction.

This is why "let the market decide" is not a neutral position. Markets do decide, but the decision is shaped by whoever can afford to shape the rules. Without deliberate counter-pressure, capture is the default destination of released value.

## The likely capture zones

During the AI transition, capture will concentrate around seven areas:

Compute infrastructure. Chip supply and fabrication. Energy and grid access. Data access and proprietary training corpora. Distribution channels and platforms. Regulatory and liability frameworks. Identity, trust, and certification layers.

These zones matter because intelligence can become technically cheaper while access remains structurally gated. A world where advice, education, or analysis are cheap to produce but expensive to access is not an open transition. It is a rerouted scarcity model.

---

## Four capture patterns

Capture does not operate through a single mechanism. Four patterns recur across different domains.

**Infrastructure capture.** When production costs fall, control shifts to the pipes. The strategy is to control compute, distribution, and deployment rails, then price access to the infrastructure layer. In the four-scarcity framework, this is the migration of rent extraction from cognitive scarcity (which AI has dissolved) to physical scarcity (which persists).

**Complexity reintroduction.** As AI simplifies a task, new complexity appears nearby to preserve intermediaries. Expanded compliance burdens, new liability requirements, procedural density, and certification overheads, particularly in law, healthcare, and financial services. This is the defence of manufactured scarcity after cognitive scarcity has been compressed.

**Residual position capture.** When mass-market deflation becomes difficult to resist, incumbents retreat to the high-complexity tail and redefine it as the "real" version of the service. Sometimes this residual is genuine human scarcity. Sometimes it preserves authority beyond what quality requires: manufactured scarcity dressed in the language of human scarcity.

**Physical layer capture.** As cognitive and manufactured scarcity weaken, rent extraction migrates to physical bottlenecks. This is often the least visible form and one of the most consequential. If the transition depends on narrow physical chokepoints, the scarcity game survives while digital services appear to deflate.

---

## The test

A simple test for whether you are seeing healthy deflation or capture:

If production cost falls and access cost falls with it, you are seeing deflation flowing into commons.

If production cost falls but access cost stays the same or rises, you are seeing capture.

If new complexity appears around a task that AI has simplified, you are seeing complexity reintroduction.

If the savings accrue to platform owners rather than to users or producers, you are seeing infrastructure capture.

The key test: if only the producer benefits but the user does not, you may be seeing capture in a more efficient form.

---

## Why this matters now

Shoshana Zuboff's analysis of surveillance capitalism demonstrated how the efficiency gains of the internet era were captured rather than distributed. The AI transition faces the same risk at larger scale and faster speed.

Understanding capture as a structural tendency rather than an aberration is the first step toward designing against it. If capture is treated as an accident, the counter-strategies will be naive. If it is treated as a default outcome that requires deliberate counter-pressure, there is a chance of directing released value toward broader access rather than new tollbooths.

The central problem of the AI transition is not automation. It is capture.
