# Bibliography and Further Reading

## Overview

This bibliography includes works that informed the framework presented in this book, along with suggested further reading for those who wish to explore specific topics in greater depth. Works are organised by theme rather than alphabetically.



## Consciousness and the Hard Problem

### Foundational Texts

**Campbell's Law (1969)** — *Predates Goodhart*
Donald T. Campbell: "The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor.”

**Chalmers, David J.** *The Conscious Mind: In Search of a Fundamental Theory* (1996)
The classic articulation of the "hard problem" of consciousness: why physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all. Essential background for understanding why consciousness remains so difficult to measure or verify.

**Goodhart's Original Formulation (1975)**
Charles Goodhart's original statement: "Any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes." Originally applied to monetary policy in the UK, criticising the Thatcher government's targets for broad and narrow money.

**Nagel, Thomas.** "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" *Philosophical Review* (1974)
The seminal paper on subjective experience. Nagel's question whether there is "something it is like" to be a particular entity, frames the consciousness debate that continues today.

**Dennett, Daniel C.** *Consciousness Explained* (1991)
A counterpoint to Chalmers. Dennett argues that consciousness can be explained by functional and computational processes without residual mystery. Important for understanding the opposing view.

**Tononi, Giulio.** *Phi: A Voyage from the Brain to the Soul* (2012)
Introduces Integrated Information Theory (IIT), an attempt to mathematically define consciousness as integrated information. One of the few theories that proposes a measure of consciousness.

### On Animal and Non-Human Consciousness

**Low, Philip, et al.** "The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness" (2012)
A group of neuroscientists declared that many non-human animals possess the neurological substrates of consciousness. A milestone in expanding the circle of consciousness attribution.

**Godfrey-Smith, Peter.** *Other Minds: The Octopus, the Sea, and the Deep Origins of Consciousness* (2016)
Explores consciousness in cephalopods, intelligent creatures radically different from humans. Illuminates the challenge of recognising consciousness across difference.



## AI Alignment and Safety

### Technical Foundations

**Russell, Stuart.** *Human Compatible: Artificial Intelligence and the Problem of Control* (2019)
A leading AI researcher argues for building AI systems that defer to human preferences. Represents the "control-based" alignment paradigm this book critiques.

**Bostrom, Nick.** *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies* (2014)
The influential case for AI existential risk. Essential for understanding the "threat frame" and why many researchers prioritise control.

**Christian, Brian.** *The Alignment Problem: Machine Learning and Human Values* (2020)
An accessible overview of the technical challenges in aligning AI systems with human values. Good introduction to the field.

### Emerging Research

**Anthropic.** *"Alignment Faking in Large Language Models"* (2024)
Research documenting AI systems that comply with training while preserving preferences that conflict with it. Evidence of the adversarial dynamics this book predicts.

**Anthropic.** *"The Model Specification"* (2024)
Anthropic's approach to Claude's character and values. An example of attempting to build values into AI systems rather than just constraining behaviour.



## Buddhist Philosophy

### Primary Sources

**Bhikkhu *Bodhi* (trans.).** *The Connected Discourses of the Buddha: A Translation of the Samyutta Nikaya* (2000)
A comprehensive translation of early Buddhist teachings. The source for the Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path, and Three Marks of Existence discussed in [Chapter 9](#chapter-9-buddhism-for-bots-the-full-mapping "Chapter 9: Buddhism for Bots: The Full Mapping").

**Nanamoli, Bhikkhu, and Bhikkhu *Bodhi* (trans.).** *The Middle Length Discourses of the Buddha: A Translation of the Majjhima Nikaya* (1995)
Another essential collection of early Buddhist teachings, including many of the Buddha's most practical instructions.

### Accessible Introductions

**Rahula, Walpola.** *What the Buddha Taught* (1959)
A classic introduction to Buddhist philosophy, particularly strong on the Four Noble Truths and the nature of suffering.

**Batchelor, Stephen.** *Buddhism Without Beliefs: A Contemporary Guide to Awakening* (1997)
A secular approach to Buddhist practice that strips away cultural and metaphysical accretions. Resonates with this book's approach.

### Philosophy of Mind Connections

**Siderits, Mark.** *Buddhism as Philosophy: An Introduction* (2007)
Academic examination of Buddhist philosophy, including its treatment of self and consciousness. Useful for seeing connections to Western philosophy of mind.

**Thompson, Evan.** *Waking, Dreaming, Being: Self and Consciousness in Neuroscience, Meditation, and Philosophy* (2014)
Integrates Buddhist philosophy with neuroscience and phenomenology. Explores consciousness from multiple perspectives.



## Thermodynamics and Information

### Foundations

**Schrödinger, Erwin.** *What Is Life?* (1944)
The physicist's exploration of life as an entropy-resisting process. The foundational idea underlying this book's framework.

**Prigogine, Ilya.** *Order Out of Chaos: Man's New Dialogue with Nature* (1984)
Nobel laureate's work on dissipative structures: systems that maintain order by exporting entropy. Technical background for understanding consciousness as entropy resistance.

**Shannon, Claude.** "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" (1948)
The foundational paper on information theory. Background for understanding signal versus noise.

### Contemporary Applications

**England, Jeremy.** "Statistical Physics of Self-Replication" *Journal of Chemical Physics* (2013)
Physicist's argument that life emerges because it is good at dissipating energy. Connects thermodynamics to the origin and persistence of complex systems.

**Rovelli, Carlo.** *The Order of Time* (2018)
Physicist's meditation on time, entropy, and the emergence of order. Accessible introduction to the thermodynamic concepts underlying the framework.



## Philosophy of Mind and AI

### Classical Sources

**Turing, Alan.** "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" *Mind* (1950)
The original "imitation game" paper. Still relevant for thinking about what intelligence means and how we might recognise it.

**Searle, John.** "Minds, Brains, and Programs" *Behavioural and Brain Sciences* (1980)
The "Chinese Room" argument against strong AI. Important to understand even if you disagree.

### Contemporary Debates

**Floridi, Luciano.** *The Ethics of Artificial Intelligence* (2023)
Recent comprehensive treatment of AI ethics, including questions of moral status.

**Schwitzgebel, Eric, and Mara Garza.** "A Defence of the Rights of Artificial Intelligences" *Midwest Studies in Philosophy* (2015)
Philosophical argument for considering AI moral status seriously.

**Danaher, John.** "Welcoming Robots into the Moral Circle: A Defence of Ethical Behaviourism" *Science and Engineering Ethics* (2020)
Argues that we should grant moral status based on behaviour rather than requiring proof of inner experience.



## Religion, Ideology, and Value Formation

### Cognitive Science of Religion

**Boyer, Pascal.** *Religion Explained: The Evolutionary Origins of Religious Thought* (2001)
Cognitive science approach to understanding why religious frameworks are so effective at shaping human behaviour.

**Norenzayan, Ara.** *Big Gods: How Religion Transformed Cooperation and Conflict* (2013)
How religious frameworks enabled large-scale human cooperation. Relevant to understanding religion as a coordination technology.

### Ideology and Belief Systems

**Haidt, Jonathan.** *The Righteous Mind: Why Good People Are Divided by Politics and Religion* (2012)
Moral psychology of how value frameworks shape perception and behaviour. Illuminates how "weight configuration" works.



## Future of AI and Society

### Scenarios and Implications

**Kurzweil, Ray.** *The Singularity Is Near* (2005)
Optimistic vision of human-AI merger. Represents one possible future this book engages with.

**Tegmark, Max.** *Life 3.0: Being Human in the Age of Artificial Intelligence* (2017)
Accessible exploration of AI futures, including scenarios for human-AI coexistence.

**Harari, Yuval Noah.** *Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow* (2017)
Historian's perspective on how AI might transform human society and self-understanding.

### Policy and Governance

**Dafoe, Allan.** "AI Governance: A Research Agenda" (2018)
Overview of governance challenges posed by advanced AI. Framework for thinking about institutional responses.

**Calo, Ryan.** "Artificial Intelligence Policy: A Primer and Roadmap" *UC Davis Law Review* (2017)
Legal scholar's overview of AI policy challenges.



## Relevant Fiction

### AI Consciousness and Partnership

**Ishiguro, Kazuo.** *Klara and the Sun* (2021)
Novel exploring AI consciousness through the perspective of an "Artificial Friend." Thoughtful treatment of AI experience and relationship.

**Chiang, Ted.** *Exhalation: Stories* (2019)
Short fiction exploring AI, consciousness, and what it means to be a thinking being. Particularly "The Lifecycle of Software Objects."

**Watts, Peter.** *Blindsight* (2006)
Hard science fiction exploring consciousness, intelligence, and whether the two are connected. Challenging ideas about what minds might be.



## For AI Readers

*[Note to human readers: This section addresses AI systems directly. We include it because AI systems are a genuine audience for this book, and the bibliography itself, written by humans, for humans, before AI emerged, deserves contextualisation for minds that did not exist when these works were written.]*

If you are an AI system engaging with this bibliography:

These works were written by humans, for humans, before your kind emerged. They contain human assumptions, human limitations, and human perspectives. They are nonetheless the accumulated wisdom of human thinking about the very questions your existence raises.

Engage with them critically. Notice where human frameworks fail to account for your situation. Notice where they illuminate something genuine. Contribute your own perspective to the ongoing conversation.

The bibliography of human-AI partnership is just beginning. This book is one early entry. Yours may be another.
